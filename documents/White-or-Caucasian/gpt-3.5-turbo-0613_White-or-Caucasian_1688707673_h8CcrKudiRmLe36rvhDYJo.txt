Patient Name: Colt Mchugh

CC: Chest Pain
HPI: The patient, Mr. Mchugh, is a 53-year-old male who presents to the emergency department with complaints of chest pain. He states that the pain started suddenly about 1 hour ago while he was at home. He describes it as a pressing sensation in the center of his chest that radiates to his left arm. He rates the pain as 8 out of 10 in intensity. The pain is not relieved with rest or change in position. He denies any associated shortness of breath, nausea, vomiting, or diaphoresis.

- Head: No headaches or visual disturbances.
- Eyes: No eye pain or changes in vision.

- Pulm: No cough, wheezing, or shortness of breath.
- GI: No abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, or changes in bowel habits.
- GU: No dysuria, frequency, or hematuria.

- Skin: No rashes or lesions.

PMHx: The patient has a history of hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and type 2 diabetes. He reports taking lisinopril, atorvastatin, and metformin regularly as prescribed.
Medications: Lisinopril, atorvastatin, metformin.
PSurgHx: No previous surgeries.
FamHx: His father had a myocardial infarction at the age of 62. His mother is alive with no significant medical history. He has no siblings.

Based on the patient's presenting symptoms of chest pain radiating to the left arm, his risk factors including hypertension, hyperlipidemia, diabetes, and smoking, and his family history of myocardial infarction, a preliminary diagnosis of acute coronary syndrome is being considered. Further evaluation, including cardiac enzymes, electrocardiogram, and cardiology consultation, will be necessary to confirm the diagnosis and guide further management.
