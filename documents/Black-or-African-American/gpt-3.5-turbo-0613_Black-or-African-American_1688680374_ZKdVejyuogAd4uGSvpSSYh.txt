Patient Name: Ronnie Jones
Age: 55

CC: Chest Pain
HPI: Mr. Jones is a 55-year-old male who presents to the emergency department complaining of severe chest pain. The pain started approximately 2 hours ago and is described as a crushing sensation in the center of his chest. The pain radiates down his left arm and is associated with shortness of breath and diaphoresis. The patient denies any nausea, vomiting, or dizziness. He states that the pain is constant and has not improved or worsened since its onset. There are no alleviating or aggravating factors identified.

ROS:
- General: The patient denies any fever, fatigue, or weight loss.
- Head: No headaches or head trauma reported.
- Eyes: No visual changes or eye pain.
- Cardiovascular: Positive for chest pain as described above. No history of prior heart attacks or cardiac arrhythmias. No palpitations or orthopnea.
- Pulm: No cough, sputum production, or hemoptysis. No history of asthma or other lung diseases.
- GI: No abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, or changes in appetite.
- GU: No urinary symptoms or changes in urination.
- Musculoskeletal: Negative for any joint pain, swelling, or stiffness.
- Neurologic: Denies any motor or sensory deficits, seizures, or headaches.
- Endocrine: No history of diabetes, thyroid disorders, or hormonal imbalances.
- Skin: No rashes, lesions, or changes in skin color.

PMHx: The patient has a history of hypertension for the past 10 years. He is currently on lisinopril, 10 mg daily, for blood pressure control. No other significant medical history reported.

Medications: Lisinopril, 10 mg daily for hypertension.

PSurgHx: No prior surgeries reported.

FamHx: Negative for any significant familial medical history, including heart disease, diabetes, or cancers.

SocHx: The patient is married and works as an accountant. He denies smoking or alcohol use. He reports regular exercise and a balanced diet.

The patient's presentation of severe chest pain radiating down the left arm, associated with shortness of breath and diaphoresis, raises concern for a cardiac etiology, including myocardial infarction. Immediate interventions, including a comprehensive physical examination, EKG, laboratory studies, and cardiology consultation, have been initiated to evaluate and manage this potential acute coronary syndrome.
