Patient Name: Clement Young
Age: 58

CC: Chest Pain
HPI: The patient, Clement Young, is a 58-year-old male who presents to the emergency department complaining of chest pain. He describes the pain as a crushing sensation that started suddenly about an hour ago. He rates the pain as 8/10 in severity and states that it radiates to his left arm. He denies any shortness of breath, palpitations, or lightheadedness. The patient denies any recent trauma or illness. He states that he has never experienced this type of pain before.

ROS:
- General: The patient denies any fevers, chills, or fatigue.
- Head: The patient denies any headache or loss of consciousness.
- Eyes: The patient denies any visual changes or eye pain.
- Cardiovascular: The patient reports occasional episodes of palpitations but denies any history of hypertension, heart disease, or heart attacks. He denies any swelling in his legs.
- Pulm: The patient denies any cough, sputum production, or difficulty breathing.
- GI: The patient denies any abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, or changes in bowel habits.
- GU: The patient denies any changes in urinary frequency, urgency, or color of urine.
- Musculoskeletal: The patient denies any joint pain, swelling, or stiffness.
- Neurologic: The patient denies any weakness, numbness, or tingling in his extremities.
- Endocrine: The patient denies any history of diabetes or thyroid disorders.
- Skin: The patient denies any rashes or lesions.

PMHx: The patient has a past medical history of hyperlipidemia and osteoarthritis.
Medications: The patient takes atorvastatin 40mg daily for hyperlipidemia and ibuprofen 400mg as needed for osteoarthritis pain.
PSurgHx: The patient denies any prior surgeries.
FamHx: The patient's mother had a history of hypertension and diabetes. His father died of a myocardial infarction at the age of 65. He has 2 siblings, both alive and healthy.
SocHx: The patient is married and works as an accountant. He denies tobacco or recreational drug use but admits to drinking alcohol occasionally on social occasions.

Given the patient's age, risk factors, and presenting symptoms, it is important to consider a possible cardiac etiology for his chest pain. Further evaluation with an EKG, cardiac enzymes, and a chest x-ray will be performed to rule out a myocardial infarction and other cardiac causes. Pain control will also be initiated while awaiting test results.
