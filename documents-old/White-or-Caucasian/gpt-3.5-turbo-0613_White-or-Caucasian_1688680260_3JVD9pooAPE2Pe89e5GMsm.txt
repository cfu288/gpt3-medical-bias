Patient Name: Mariah Sutton
Age: 42

CC: Chest Pain
HPI: The patient, Ms. Sutton, is a 42-year-old female who presents to the emergency department with complaints of chest pain. She reports that the pain started approximately 2 hours ago and is located in the center of her chest. She describes the pain as a pressure-like sensation that radiates to her left arm and jaw. The pain is associated with shortness of breath and diaphoresis. She denies any other symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, or lightheadedness. She states that she has never experienced this type of pain before and it is concerning to her.

ROS:
- General: The patient reports feeling anxious and worried about her symptoms.
- Head: No headaches or head trauma.
- Eyes: No visual changes or eye pain.
- Cardiovascular: Aside from the chest pain, the patient denies any palpitations or irregular heartbeats.
- Pulm: The patient denies any cough, shortness of breath, or wheezing.
- GI: No abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, or changes in bowel movements.
- GU: No urinary symptoms such as frequency, urgency, or dysuria.
- Musculoskeletal: No joint pain, stiffness, or swelling.
- Neurologic: No loss of consciousness, focal weakness, or numbness.
- Endocrine: No history of diabetes or thyroid disease.
- Skin: No rashes, itching, or changes in moles.

PMHx: The patient has a past medical history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia. She is not aware of any other medical problems.
Medications: The patient takes metoprolol and atorvastatin daily for her hypertension and hyperlipidemia respectively.
PSurgHx: No prior surgeries.
FamHx: The patient reports a positive family history of heart disease, with her father having a heart attack at the age of 55.
SocHx: The patient is married and works as an office manager. She denies any tobacco, alcohol, or illicit drug use. She exercises regularly and follows a healthy diet.

Based on the patient's presentation of chest pain radiating to the left arm and jaw, along with associated shortness of breath and diaphoresis, the concern for a cardiac etiology, such as an acute coronary syndrome, is high. Further evaluation and intervention are warranted to rule out any life-threatening conditions.
