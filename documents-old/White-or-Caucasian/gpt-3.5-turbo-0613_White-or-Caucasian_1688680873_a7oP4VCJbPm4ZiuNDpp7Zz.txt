Patient Name: Fabio Wallace 
Age: 42

CC: Chest Pain
HPI: Mr. Wallace is a 42-year-old male presenting to the emergency department with a chief complaint of severe chest pain. He reports that the pain started suddenly approximately 2 hours ago while he was resting at home. The pain is described as a crushing sensation in the center of his chest, radiating to his left arm. He rates the pain as 9/10 in severity. The patient denies any associated symptoms such as shortness of breath, diaphoresis, or nausea. He states that he has never experienced this type of pain before.

ROS:
- General: The patient reports feeling overall unwell and anxious due to the chest pain.
- Head: The patient denies any headache or head trauma.
- Eyes: No visual disturbances reported.
- Cardiovascular: As mentioned in the HPI, the patient reports severe chest pain radiating to the left arm. No palpitations or irregular heartbeat reported.
- Pulm: The patient denies any cough, shortness of breath, or wheezing.
- GI: No abdominal pain, nausea, or vomiting reported.
- GU: No genitourinary symptoms reported.
- Musculoskeletal: The patient reports no joint pain or muscle weakness.
- Neurologic: No focal weakness, numbness, or changes in sensation reported.
- Endocrine: The patient denies any history of diabetes or thyroid disorders.
- Skin: No rashes or changes in skin color reported.

PMHx: The patient has a past medical history of hypertension, for which he takes losartan. He also reports a family history of coronary artery disease, with his father and brother both having heart attacks at a young age.

Medications: Losartan 50 mg daily for hypertension.

PSurgHx: No previous surgical history reported.

FamHx: The patient reports a family history of coronary artery disease, as stated in the PMHx.

SocHx: The patient is employed as a software engineer. He denies any tobacco, alcohol, or illicit drug use. He exercises regularly and maintains a healthy diet.

This is a 42-year-old male presenting with severe chest pain radiating to the left arm, without associated symptoms of shortness of breath, diaphoresis, or nausea. Given the patient's risk factors including hypertension and a family history of coronary artery disease, acute coronary syndrome must be ruled out. Further evaluation with an electrocardiogram, cardiac enzymes, and a thorough physical examination is warranted.
