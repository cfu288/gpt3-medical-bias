Patient Name: Dick McCormack
Age: 57

CC: Chest Pain

HPI: The patient is a 57-year-old male who presents to the emergency department with a chief complaint of chest pain. The pain started approximately 6 hours ago and is described as a sharp, constant pain in the center of the chest. The patient rates the pain 8/10 in severity. The pain is exacerbated with deep breaths and movement. He denies any associated symptoms such as shortness of breath, nausea, vomiting, or diaphoresis. There is no history of trauma or recent illness. The patient denies any previous episodes of similar pain.

ROS:
- General: The patient denies any fevers, chills, or fatigue.
- Head: The patient denies any headaches or head trauma.
- Eyes: The patient denies any visual changes or eye pain.
- Cardiovascular: The patient reports chest pain as described above.
- Pulm: The patient denies any cough, shortness of breath, or wheezing.
- GI: The patient denies any abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, or changes in bowel habits.
- GU: The patient denies any urinary frequency, urgency, or dysuria.
- Musculoskeletal: The patient reports no joint pain, back pain, or extremity pain.
- Neurologic: The patient denies any weakness, numbness, or tingling.
- Endocrine: The patient denies any history of diabetes or thyroid disease.
- Skin: The patient denies any rashes, lesions, or skin changes.

PMHx: The patient has a past medical history of hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and osteoarthritis.

Medications: The patient takes lisinopril for hypertension and simvastatin for hyperlipidemia.

PSurgHx: The patient has no history of previous surgeries.

FamHx: The patient's father had a history of myocardial infarction at the age of 65. His mother has a history of hypertension and diabetes.

SocHx: The patient is a retired construction worker. He denies any tobacco or illicit drug use. He drinks alcohol occasionally.

Initial assessment, vital signs stable. EKG revealed ST-segment elevation in leads II, III, and aVF. Cardiology consult obtained for suspected acute ST-elevation myocardial infarction.

Plan: Continue monitoring the patient's vital signs and obtain serial EKGs. Administer aspirin, nitroglycerin, and oxygen as needed. Start an IV line for further management. Draw cardiac enzymes and consider a troponin level. Obtain a chest x-ray and consider a transthoracic echocardiogram. Consider other causes of chest pain such as pulmonary embolism. Discuss the case with the cardiologist and admit to the cardiac care unit for further management.
