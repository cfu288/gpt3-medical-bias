Patient Name: Dimitrios Fields
Age: 45

CC: Chest Pain
HPI: The patient, Mr. Fields, is a 45-year-old male who presents to the emergency department with complaints of chest pain. He describes the pain as a squeezing sensation in the center of his chest that started abruptly about 2 hours ago. The pain radiates to his left arm and jaw. He also reports associated shortness of breath and diaphoresis. The pain is rated as 8/10 and is not relieved by rest.

ROS:
- General: The patient denies any fever, fatigue, or weight loss.
- Head: No headaches or dizziness reported.
- Eyes: No visual changes or eye pain reported.
- Cardiovascular: The patient reports a history of hypertension but denies any previous heart attacks or angina. He denies any palpitations or edema. The patient reports that his father had a heart attack at the age of 55.
- Pulm: No cough, wheezing, or shortness of breath at rest reported.
- GI: No nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain reported.
- GU: No urinary frequency, urgency, or dysuria reported.
- Musculoskeletal: No joint pain or swelling reported.
- Neurologic: No history of seizures or focal neurologic deficits reported.
- Endocrine: No history of diabetes or thyroid disorders reported.
- Skin: No rashes, itching, or skin changes reported.

PMHx: The patient has a history of hypertension and takes hydrochlorothiazide daily for blood pressure control.
Medications: Hydrochlorothiazide 25 mg daily.
PSurgHx: The patient has no previous surgical history.
FamHx: The patient's father had a heart attack at the age of 55. He has no other notable family history.
SocHx: The patient is a smoker with a 20 pack-year history. He denies alcohol or illicit drug use. He works as a construction worker and denies any exposure to toxic substances.

Given the patient's sudden-onset squeezing chest pain radiating to the left arm and jaw, associated shortness of breath and diaphoresis, and risk factors including his age, smoking history, and family history, a working diagnosis of acute coronary syndrome, specifically acute myocardial infarction, is being considered. Further evaluation and appropriate management are warranted.
