CC: Chest Pain
HPI: The patient, a 42-year-old female, presents to the emergency department with complaints of sharp, stabbing chest pain that started suddenly approximately 30 minutes ago. The pain is located in the center of her chest and is radiating to her left arm. She describes the pain as 8 out of 10 in intensity. She denies any associated symptoms such as shortness of breath, diaphoresis, or nausea. She reports no recent exertion or trauma. The pain has remained constant and has not improved with rest or over-the-counter pain relievers. There is no history of similar episodes in the past.

- Head: No headache or head trauma
- Eyes: No visual changes or eye pain

- Neurologic: No syncope, dizziness, or focal weakness
- Endocrine: No polyuria, polydipsia, or heat/cold intolerance
- Skin: No rashes or skin lesions

PMHx: The patient has a past medical history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, both of which are well-controlled on medications. She has no history of diabetes, thyroid disease, or any other chronic medical conditions.

Medications: The patient is taking lisinopril 20 mg daily for hypertension and atorvastatin 40 mg daily for hyperlipidemia.

PSurgHx: The patient has no history of any surgeries.

FamHx: The patient's mother has a history of hypertension and her father passed away from a myocardial infarction at the age of 55. There is no history of any other significant medical conditions in the family.

SocHx: The patient works as a teacher and denies any tobacco or illicit drug use. She drinks alcohol occasionally but denies any excessive alcohol consumption. The patient is married and lives with her spouse and two children. She reports no recent travel or exposure to sick contacts.

The patient's chief complaint of sudden-onset chest pain radiating to the left arm and associated with a history of hypertension and a family history of myocardial infarction is concerning for a cardiac etiology. Given the patient's symptoms, further evaluation and appropriate management is warranted to rule out acute coronary syndrome.
